Green light for kidney disease drug welcomed by CH consultant

CH’s Dr Richard Sandford has welcomed NHS approval of a drug to tackle kidney disease.

The National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) has approved the use of Jinarc (tolvaptan) on the NHS in England and Wales following a series of trials with patients.

Until now many of the 60,000 patients with ADPKD (autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease) in the UK did not have a single treatment option to delay and stall disease progression.

ADPKD is an inherited condition that causes small, fluid-filled sacs called cysts to develop in the kidneys.

The condition can cause a wide range of problems including abdominal pain, high blood pressure and kidney stones.
Though children are born with the condition, it is not often noticeable until someone reaches 30 years old.
For many people, kidney failure at a young age can be daunting prospect.

Because the disease is genetic, there is a one in two chance of it being passed to children of a parent with the gene. One British family holds the world record for the number of members having a transplant. So far, across three generations, 11 have had new donor kidneys.

Over a three-year period, a study carried out by NICE revealed that taking tolvaptan reduced the volume of the disease-ridden kidney by nearly half.

Dr Richard Sandford, a consultant clinical geneticist at Addenbrooke's Hospital, Cambridge, said: “This drug will give us the opportunity to help some patients to reduce the number of symptoms they experience and potentially slow their progression towards kidney failure.

For many people with ADPKD, kidney failure at a relatively young age has been a daunting prospect which we've been unable to guard against.
They have often seen other close family members require dialysis or a kidney transplant.
